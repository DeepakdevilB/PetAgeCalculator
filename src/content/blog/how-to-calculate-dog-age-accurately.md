For decades, pet owners have relied on a simple mathematical formula to determine their dog's age: *just multiply by seven*. 

Unfortunately, science has proven this age-old myth to be completely inaccurate. Dogs age much faster than humans during their first two years of life, and their aging process slows down significantly afterward. Furthermore, the size and breed of your dog drastically impact their lifespan and aging curve.

## Why the "Multiply by 7" Rule is Wrong

The 7:1 ratio was likely created as a marketing tool in the 1950s to encourage owners to bring their dogs to the vet once a year (which would theoretically be every "seven years" for the dog).

In reality:
1. **The First Year:** A 1-year-old dog is generally equivalent to a 15-year-old human. They reach sexual maturity and physical adulthood incredibly fast.
2. **The Second Year:** Year two adds about 9 human years, making a 2-year-old dog approximately 24 in human years.
3. **Subsequent Years:** After year two, each calendar year equals roughly 4 to 5 human years, depending on the dog's size.

## Size Matters: The Lifespan Factor

The most crucial factor in calculating a dog's age is their adult weight. Unlike almost every other species on Earth, smaller dogs live significantly longer than larger dogs.

*   **Toy & Small Breeds (under 25 lbs):** Can live 15-18+ years. They age slower in their later years.
*   **Medium Breeds (25-50 lbs):** Typically live 10-14 years.
*   **Large Breeds (50-100 lbs):** Have a lifespan of 9-12 years.
*   **Giant Breeds (100+ lbs):** Sadly, giant breeds like Great Danes age very quickly, often considered seniors by age 5 or 6, with lifespans around 7-10 years.
